Program Overview

What This Program Is - and What It Isn't

The Enterprise AI Systems program prepares business professionals to design, deploy, and govern autonomous AI systems in organizational contexts. This is not a computer science degree. You will not be asked to implement backpropagation, derive attention from first principles, or compete with ML engineers on their home turf. What you will be asked to do is harder in its own way: understand these systems well enough to make strategic decisions about them, build them well enough to put them to real use, and govern them well enough that they don't blow up in your face.

The student this program was designed for comes in with strong quantitative intuition - you can read a regression, interpret a confidence interval, think clearly about causality. You've touched Python. You understand that data pipelines exist and roughly what they do. You may have experimented with ChatGPT or Claude. What you haven't done is built an agent that actually runs in production, makes decisions autonomously, and talks to real systems. That's what this program changes.

By the end of two semesters, you will have:

  • Built and deployed at least two functioning multi-agent systems from scratch
  • Designed the data infrastructure that powers AI decision-making at enterprise scale
  • Navigated the organizational change management challenges of AI adoption
  • Understood and applied the EU AI Act (including 2025-2027 implementation timeline), US executive orders, and corporate governance frameworks
  • Assessed the security posture of an agentic system and red-teamed your own work
  • Analyzed the macroeconomic implications of automation on labor markets and firm strategy
  • Delivered a real capstone project - designed, built, deployed, and defended before an industry panel
A note on the ramp: This program assumes you are starting from near-zero on the hands-on technical side. Semester 1 is designed to build you up carefully - not to throw you into the deep end. Every technical course has an explicit "before week 1" section with specific resources and estimated hours. The first four weeks of every technical course assume you haven't done this before. You will feel lost in weeks 2-4. That's not a bug. Push through it - the payoff is real.
What Makes This Different From an MBA

An MBA gives you frameworks for managing what already exists. This program gives you the capability to build what doesn't exist yet. The difference is whether you're optimizing the 20th-century firm or architecting the 21st-century one. MBAs learn to analyze companies that use AI. EAIS graduates learn to build the AI systems those companies run on.

What Makes This Different From a CS Degree

CS degrees optimize for depth-first technical mastery. You learn to build any system from scratch. EAIS optimizes for deployment judgment - knowing when to use which tool, how to evaluate tradeoffs, what the organizational and legal consequences of a technical decision are. A CS grad knows how to build a transformer. An EAIS grad knows whether building a custom transformer is the right call, what data infrastructure it needs to operate, how to hire the team that maintains it, and how to explain the liability exposure to your legal department.

Prerequisites: What You Should Know Before Day One

The program assumes the following incoming baseline. If any of these feel shaky, the specific remediation resources are listed in each course's "Before Week 1" section.

  • Python basics: variables, functions, loops, conditionals, importing libraries. You don't need to be good - you need to not be lost when you see a for loop.
  • Data intuition: you understand what a dataframe is, what a CSV looks like, what an API endpoint returns. Economics training covers this.
  • Statistical literacy: mean, variance, regression, probability - all covered by an econ BA.
  • Business context: understanding of firm strategy, organizational structure, and basic accounting - covered by your BBA.
  • Curiosity and tolerance for ambiguity: this field is changing weekly. You will regularly have to figure things out with incomplete documentation.
Dean's Statement

Why This Major Exists Now

There is a specific kind of historical moment when a new capability appears that is genuinely general-purpose - not incremental, not a niche improvement to an existing process, but a new substrate that changes what is possible across every domain of economic life simultaneously. The steam engine was one such moment. Electrification was another. The internet was a third. We are living through a fourth.

The reason this program exists in 2026 - rather than 2018, when transformer models were first published, or 2022, when ChatGPT made language models legible to the public - is that the capability has finally crossed the threshold from impressive demonstration to reliable deployment. Agentic AI systems - systems that can perceive their environment, make decisions, take actions, use tools, and pursue goals over extended time horizons without step-by-step human instruction - are no longer research artifacts. They are running in production. They are handling customer service at scale. They are writing and executing code. They are managing investment portfolios. They are coordinating procurement chains. They are writing legal memos, drafting regulatory comments, and filing forms.

The Gap That This Program Fills

Here is the problem: the people who understand how to build these systems are almost entirely computer scientists and ML engineers. The people who will be responsible for deploying them, governing them, understanding their economic consequences, and managing the organizations they transform are almost entirely business professionals, economists, lawyers, and policy analysts. These two populations currently speak almost entirely different languages. The result is predictable: AI systems get built by people who don't understand the organizational context they're entering, and deployment decisions get made by people who don't understand the systems they're deploying. Both sides fail.

The Enterprise AI Systems program exists to close this gap. Not by turning economists into ML engineers - but by creating a new kind of professional who can fluently inhabit both worlds. Someone who can read a LangGraph state machine, understand what it's doing, and also understand the organizational politics of deploying it. Someone who can design a knowledge architecture that actually reflects how a company's information flows, not just what fits neatly in a vector database.

What Happens to Students Who Don't Get This Education

The honest answer is: nothing catastrophic in the short term. The graduates who skip this will still get jobs. Analytics roles will still exist in 2027. The problem is trajectory. The analyst who cannot engage with AI systems at a technical level will find their role increasingly constrained - they will be the person who interprets outputs that agents produced, rather than the person who decides what the agents do. That is a significant difference in leverage, compensation, and career ceiling. The McKinsey Global Institute's 2024 automation report estimated that by 2030, 60-70% of hours worked in analytical and knowledge-work roles will be augmented or substituted by AI systems. "Augmented" means the human is directing the system. "Substituted" means the human is gone. Which side of that line you land on depends almost entirely on whether you can operate at the level of the system.

The students who go through this program will not be the people who describe what AI is doing to their industry. They will be the people designing it.

That is why this program exists. That is why now.

Line-by-Line: Your First Agent (Week 7 Lab)

This is what Week 7's lab code looks like. Every line is explained so you understand what's happening before you run it.

import openai                    # The OpenAI Python library - lets you talk to LLMs via API
import json                      # Built-in Python library - handles JSON formatting

client = openai.OpenAI()         # Creates a client object - like opening a connection to OpenAI

# This is our "tool" - a function the agent can call
def search_web(query: str) -> str:
    # In a real system, this would call a search API
    # For this lab, we're simulating it with a hard-coded response
    return f"Search results for '{query}': [simulated results here]"

# This tells the LLM what tools it has available
# Notice it's a JSON schema - the LLM reads this description
tools = [{
    "type": "function",
    "function": {
        "name": "search_web",          # The function name the LLM will call
        "description": "Search the internet for current information",
        "parameters": {
            "type": "object",
            "properties": {
                "query": {
                    "type": "string",
                    "description": "The search query"
                }
            },
            "required": ["query"]       # The LLM MUST provide this argument
        }
    }
}]

messages = [
    {"role": "user", "content": "What is the current federal funds rate?"}
]

# First API call: LLM decides whether to use a tool
response = client.chat.completions.create(
    model="gpt-5.4",
    messages=messages,
    tools=tools                    # We pass the tools - LLM now knows it can search
)

# Check if the LLM decided to call a tool
if response.choices[0].message.tool_calls:
    tool_call = response.choices[0].message.tool_calls[0]

    # Extract the arguments the LLM chose to pass
    args = json.loads(tool_call.function.arguments)

    # Actually run the function with those arguments
    result = search_web(args["query"])

    print(f"Agent searched for: {args['query']}")
    print(f"Got back: {result}")

Professor's Note

Miles, I've taught this material to engineers, MBAs, and lawyers, and the hardest part is never the technical concepts - it's getting comfortable with not understanding everything immediately. You will run code that does something you can't fully explain in weeks 2 and 3. That's fine. Keep running it. Change things. Break it on purpose. The model of learning in this course is closer to learning a language than learning a theorem: immersion first, grammar later.

Your economics background means you can already think about optimization, tradeoffs, and emergent behavior. Transformers are an optimization problem over a very large parameter space. Agents are an emergent behavior from combining language models with tools. When you get confused, go back to first principles you already own: what is being maximized? What are the constraints? Who are the actors? What are the incentives?

The students who do well in this class are the ones who do the labs even when they feel premature. The final project is deliberately open-ended - I want to see what you find interesting enough to build. The best final projects in this class have been: an agent that monitors FRED data and drafts economic briefings (that one was excellent), a multi-turn negotiation simulator for procurement, and a research agent for competitive intelligence. You don't need to reinvent anything. You need to build something real that you care about.

Enterprise Knowledge Architecture

Organizations are drowning in information and starving for knowledge. This course addresses the architectural challenge of structuring organizational knowledge so that AI agents - and humans - can retrieve, reason over, and act on it reliably. Students build real retrieval-augmented generation systems, work with knowledge graphs, and learn the critical but underappreciated skills of chunking strategy, embedding selection, and semantic search quality evaluation.

Learning Outcomes
  • Distinguish between data, information, and knowledge in the context of AI system design
  • Build a functioning RAG pipeline from document ingestion through vector embedding to query-time retrieval
  • Evaluate chunking strategies and explain how chunk size and overlap affect retrieval quality
  • Query a Neo4j knowledge graph using Cypher and integrate graph retrieval with LLM generation
  • Select embedding models for a given use case and evaluate semantic similarity quality
  • Design a knowledge architecture for a real enterprise use case, including data source mapping
  • Implement and evaluate advanced RAG techniques: HyDE, re-ranking, multi-hop retrieval
Why This Matters
RAG is arguably the most important skill in enterprise AI right now. Pure LLMs hallucinate; they have knowledge cutoff dates; they can't access your company's proprietary data. The answer to all three problems is the same: retrieval. The ability to build a system where an agent can access exactly the right piece of information at query time, from a corpus of thousands or millions of documents, is what separates toy demos from production systems. Every serious enterprise AI deployment in 2026 involves retrieval. You need to understand it cold.

As an economist: knowledge architectures are basically information market design. You're deciding what information is accessible, how quickly, at what query cost, and to which agents. The efficiency of this "market" determines the quality of every downstream decision the AI makes. That framing will serve you well.

Special Topics: Harness Engineering, MCP, and Emerging Patterns

These topics are integrated throughout the course but collected here for emphasis. They represent the frontier of enterprise AI engineering as of March 2026.

🔧 Harness Engineering (The Most Important Concept in This Program)

The emerging discipline also called "scaffolding engineering" or "systems prompting." The core thesis: the model is increasingly a commodity - what creates value is the harness you build around it. Two teams using the same Claude Sonnet 4.6 can get wildly different results based on their harness quality.

The harness is everything surrounding the model call:

  • System prompt architecture: How you structure instructions, personas, constraints, and output formats. Not "prompt engineering" in the basic sense - this is engineering the full system prompt as a specification document.
  • Tool definitions and schemas: The JSON schemas, descriptions, and parameter names you give the model determine whether it uses tools correctly. Poorly described tools = broken agents. This is covered in Week 8 but deserves its own design discipline.
  • Context window management: What goes into the context, in what order, with what priority. Summarization strategies, sliding windows, retrieval-augmented context. Context windows now range from 200K tokens (Claude, GPT-5.x) to 10M tokens (Llama 4 Scout) — but how you spend them matters more than how many you have.
  • Guardrails and output validation: Schema validation on outputs, safety classifiers, content filters, refusal handling. The layers between "model generates text" and "system takes action."
  • Error handling and retry patterns: What happens when the model returns malformed JSON? When a tool call fails? When the model hallucinates a tool that doesn't exist? Production harnesses need graceful degradation.
  • The orchestration layer: LangGraph, PydanticAI, or custom code - this is the harness framework itself. How you wire nodes, manage state, and route between agents.
  • Evaluation and iteration: How do you know if your harness is working? LangSmith traces, custom evals, A/B testing different system prompts. Harness engineering is iterative - you ship, observe, and refine.

Key resources:

  • Anthropic: System Prompt Best Practices - how to structure system prompts for Claude
  • OpenAI: Function Calling Guide - tool definition patterns (note: GPT-5.4 adds tool_search for large tool sets)
  • Anthropic: Tool Use Documentation - tool schemas, best practices, error handling

🔌 Model Context Protocol (MCP) — Industry Standard

Anthropic's open protocol for connecting AI models to external tools and data sources has become the industry standard for AI tool integration. MCP provides a standardized way for models to access databases, APIs, file systems, and other resources through a consistent interface — like USB-C for AI integrations.

The state of MCP (March 2026): Universal adoption across the ecosystem. OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, Cursor, VS Code, Windsurf, and major enterprise platforms all support MCP natively. The debate is over — MCP won. The MCP Atlas benchmark now provides standardized measurement of MCP server quality and agent capability across MCP-connected tools, making MCP proficiency a measurable, benchmarkable skill.

Key MCP concepts for this course:

  • MCP Servers: Expose tools, resources, and prompts through a standardized protocol. You'll build custom MCP servers in Week 8's harness engineering lab.
  • MCP Apps: Pre-built application packages that bundle multiple MCP servers into coherent toolkits for specific domains (e.g., data analysis, content management).
  • MCP Atlas Benchmark: Standardized evaluation of MCP server implementations — test your servers against industry benchmarks.
  • Security considerations: MCP server supply chain risks are real — a malicious MCP server has the same access as any tool the agent can call. Covered in depth in EAIS 6210.
  • MCP Official Documentation — protocol spec, SDKs, and server registry
  • Anthropic: MCP Integration Guide

🖥️ Computer Use and Browser Agents

A maturing category of agentic AI where models interact with desktop environments and web browsers directly — taking screenshots, clicking buttons, typing text. Multiple providers now offer this capability:

  • Claude Computer Use: Available for Claude Opus 4.6 and Sonnet 4.6. Controls desktop environments through screenshot capture and mouse/keyboard input. State-of-the-art on web navigation benchmarks.
  • ChatGPT Agent Mode: Built directly into ChatGPT (evolved from the earlier Operator product). Performs multi-step web tasks — booking flights, filling forms, conducting research across multiple sites — as a native capability, not a separate product.

This represents a fundamentally different integration pattern from API-based tool use: instead of structured function calls, the model perceives and acts on visual interfaces. Implications for enterprise: automated testing, legacy system integration (screen-scraping with intelligence), workflow automation on systems without APIs, and end-to-end process automation across web applications.

  • Anthropic: Computer Use Documentation — available for Claude Opus 4.6 and Sonnet 4.6
  • Note: Computer use has unique security risks (prompt injection via on-screen content). Always use sandboxed environments.

🧠 Reasoning as a Dial, Not a Model Category

Key paradigm shift (2025-2026): Reasoning is no longer a separate model category. The concept of dedicated "reasoning models" (like the old o1/o3 series) is dead — reasoning is now a configurable parameter on all frontier models. Every major provider exposes a reasoning effort dial:

  • OpenAI: reasoning.effort parameter from none to xhigh on GPT-5.x models. The o3 model line has been fully absorbed into GPT-5.x with configurable reasoning.
  • Anthropic: Extended Thinking and Adaptive Thinking on Claude 4.6 models. The model dynamically allocates "thinking" tokens based on task complexity.
  • Google: Gemini 3.1 Deep Think mode. Explicit reasoning activation for complex tasks.

How to use reasoning effort in multi-agent systems:

  • High reasoning effort (xhigh) for: complex planning, multi-step analysis, code generation, architecture decisions, quality-critical final outputs
  • Low/no reasoning effort (none/low) for: routing, classification, simple tool calls, high-volume low-latency tasks, cost-sensitive operations
  • Mixed-effort architectures: Use a fast model at low reasoning (Haiku 4.5, GPT-5 mini) for the supervisor/router, and the same or different model at high reasoning effort for the specialist that does the hard analysis. This is the standard production pattern.

The tools you use to build agent systems are themselves AI-powered — and the numbers are staggering. Windsurf reports over 1 million users and 94% AI-written code across its user base. "Vibe coding" — describing what you want in natural language and letting the AI write the code — is now a real term used by Google in official marketing. The primary AI-assisted development tools:

  • Cursor: AI-native IDE with deep codebase understanding and MCP support

The capstone is the synthesis. Students design, build, deploy, and defend a real multi-agent AI system that solves an actual business problem.
